Two Faces of Crime in San Francisco

How violent crime and property crime have diverged over two decades of data

Introduction: A Tale of Two Crime Types

When we talk about crime in San Francisco, we're usually lumping together very different phenomena. A robbery is nothing like a car theft. An assault differs fundamentally from a burglary. Yet official crime statistics often treat them as a monolithic "crime problem." This story examines what happens when we separate violent crimes from property crimes—and discover two strikingly different trends over the past two decades.

Using incident data from the San Francisco Police Department (2003-2020), we classify crimes into two broad categories:

  • Violent Crimes: Rape, Robbery, Aggravated Assault
  • Property Crimes: Larceny Theft, Burglary, Motor Vehicle Theft

The story that emerges is one of uneven progress: one type of crime has declined dramatically, while the other remains stubbornly persistent.

The Divergence: Property Crime Down, Violent Crime Stable

The chart below tells the core story. Between 2003 and 2020, property crime in San Francisco declined significantly. Larceny theft, burglary, and motor vehicle theft have become less frequent. For a city, this is the kind of outcome that typically reflects progress: better policing, community programs, economic opportunity, or some combination thereof.

But violent crime tells a different story. From 2003 to 2020, violent crimes (rape, robbery, aggravated assault) remained relatively stable—hovering around similar levels with minimal long-term change. The normalized chart (bottom panel) shows this starkly: property crime has fallen substantially from its 2003 baseline, while violent crime has fluctuated but remained relatively stable.

Why the Difference?

A Brief Reflection on Crime Types

Why might violent crime decline while property crime stagnates? Several hypotheses emerge from available research:

  • Economic Factors: Property crime may be more responsive to economic cycles (recessions → desperation → theft). Violent crime may reflect deeper social dynamics that are less swayed by short-term economic fluctuations.
  • Policing Strategy: Modern policing has invested heavily in violence reduction (hot-spot policing, gang intervention, detective work on serious crimes). Property crime often receives less focused attention.
  • Opportunity and Technology: Smartphones and portable electronics may increase theft opportunities. Online shopping (less human-to-human interaction) may reduce robbery as a proportion of property crime but not property crime overall.

None of these are definitive—this is a data story, not a causal analysis. But the data prompts important questions about where resources are directed and why certain crime types prove more tractable.

Where Does Crime Happen? Burglary Hotspots

Crime is never uniformly distributed. Certain neighborhoods concentrate burglary incidents. The map below shows all reported burglaries in San Francisco from 2018-2020—a total of 19,928 incidents. Red zones indicate clustering of incidents; cooler colors (blue, green, yellow) show lower-incident areas.

Notice the hotspots. The Tenderloin and around Market Street are prominent. So are scattered incidents across Southern Station and the Bayview neighborhoods. This is crucial context: when we talk about property crime, we're not always talking about the same neighborhoods as violent crime hotspots. Different crimes have different geographies.

Conclusion: Progress, Uneven

San Francisco's story over the past two decades is one of uneven progress. Property crime has declined significantly—a real and substantial achievement. But violent crime has remained stubbornly persistent, distributed across specific neighborhoods and districts.

This raises important questions:

  • Why have we succeeded with property crime but not violent crime?
  • Are the strategies that work for theft also effective for violence?
  • How do we allocate police and community resources to address these divergent trends?

The data doesn't answer these policy questions—that requires expertise beyond data analysis. But it frames them clearly. And that's the power of a good data story: not to declare winners and losers, but to illuminate the problems we face and prompt smarter conversations about solutions.

A Note on Data Limitations

This analysis is based on reported incidents only. Many crimes go unreported; reporting rates vary by crime type and neighborhood. A murder is almost always reported; a shoplifting often isn't. When we see violent crime as "stable," we may be seeing stable *reporting* rather than stable *actual* crime. This is why it's essential to pair data analysis with qualitative research, community feedback, and domain expertise before drawing policy conclusions.
